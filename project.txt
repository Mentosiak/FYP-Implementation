Modern computer vision systems rely heavily on large volumes of labeled data in or
der to train machine learning models. However, the manual labelling of such data is
time-consuming, expensive and can prove to be impractical at scale particularly in spe
cialized domains such as medical imaging or surveillance. This project investigates semi
supervised machine learning (SSL) as a solution to this bottleneck, leveraging small la
beled datasets alongside large amounts of unlabeled data to significantly reduce labelling
requirements while maintaining high performance. Through an extensive review of state
of-the-art SSL approaches, including classic frameworks such as Pseudo-Labeling, as well
as recent advances in methods like MixMatch, FixMatch and FlexMatch. This work de
velops a comprehensive understanding of how SSL techniques enhance representation
learning in image classification tasks.
The project applies and compares multiple SSL algorithms in a experimental pipeline
for image-based classification. Using benchmarks datasets such as CIFAR-10 with arti
f
icially limited labels, the study assesses each algorithm’s performance under consistent
conditions, examining metrics such as classification accuracy, label efficiency, conver
gence behaviour and computational cost. This will be achieved by implementing a
unified semi-supervised framework that will ensure comparison between different mod
els is fair, consistent and reproducible. This systematic comparison aims to determine
which algorithm performs best under varying label scarcity scenarios and what practical
trade-offs exist between simplicity, accuracy and computational efficiency.
Based on these findings, the most effective SSL approach for the chosen vision task will be
selected and implemented as a working application. The implementation highlights real
world applicability by demonstrating how SSL can close the performance gap between
limited-label and fully supervised training.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Deep learning has driven remarkable progress in computer vision, enabling systems to
perform image classification, object detection, and other recognition tasks with great
accuracy. These advances, however, are strongly dependent on the availability of large
volumes of labelled training data. High-quality annotation requires time, domain exper
tise, and financial resources, making large-scale labelled datasets difficult to acquire for
many real-world applications, preventing their practical implementation. Domains such
as healthcare, manufacturing, and surveillance often contain vast archives of unlabelled
images, yet annotating them at scale remains impractical. As a result, there exists
a critical bottleneck between the abundance of raw data and the scarcity of labelled
examples that modern learning algorithms require.
Semi-supervised learning (SSL) has recently emerged as a promising approach to bridge
this gap, combining a limited amount of labelled data with a much larger pool of un
labelled data to improve model performance. SSL algorithms have demonstrated sig
nificant improvements over purely supervised methods when labelled data is scarce.
In particular, recent advances such as Pseudo-Labelling, Mean Teacher, MixMatch, and
FixMatch have shown strong performance across image classification benchmarks. These
methods leverage techniques such as pseudo-labels, consistency regularisation, and aug
mentation strategies to extract meaningful learning signals from unlabelled data.
This project investigates the effectiveness of these state-of-the-art semi-supervised learn
ing algorithms within an image classification context. By conducting a comparative
analysis and finding the best model, the project aims to evaluate how SSL can reduce
reliance on labelled data while maintaining high accuracy. The study also explores
practical considerations such as computational overhead, training stability, and ease of
use, providing insights that can assist researchers and practitioners in adopting SSL
approaches for real-world computer vision tasks.

2.2.2 Unsupervised Learning
Unsupervised learning refers to machine-learning techniques that work with unlabelled
data, meaning the system has no predefined categories or correct outputs. Instead, the
goal is to discover patterns or structure within the data on its own. Common examples
include identifying clusters of similar data points or learning compact internal repre
sentations. Popular unsupervised methods include k-means clustering [20], Gaussian
mixture models [21], and representation-learning models such as autoencoders and gen
erative adversarial networks (GANs)[22] they all however work in principle in the same
way of which can be seen in the visualisation: 2.3.
2.2.3 Self-Supervised Learning
Self-supervised learning (SSL) builds on the idea of unsupervised machine learning but
introduces pretext tasks—artificial learning problems created from the data itself. For
example, an image might be modified by masking part of it, and the model learns to
predict the missing patch or an image could be rotated, and the model learns to identify

2.2.4 Semi-Supervised Learning (SSL)
Semi-supervised learning (SSL) which is the main focus of this study is a machine
learning approach that uses a combination of labelled and unlabelled data during train
ing. Since manually labelling data is often expensive and time-consuming, SSL aims to
improve model performance by learning as much as possible from the typically much
larger pool of unlabelled data. [24]
The core idea behind SSL is to extract meaningful structure from the unlabelled data,
but also ensure that the model does not learn incorrect patterns from unreliable au
tomatically generated labels (known as pseudo-labels) which happens in unsupervised
algorithms. If pseudo labels are inaccurate and reinforced repeatedly, the model may
develop confirmation bias, meaning it becomes confident in wrong predictions. There
fore, careful design and training safeguards are needed such as confidence thresholds and
consistency regularisation. [25]

2.2.5 Key Assumptions in SSL
SSL relies on several assumptions about how data is naturally organised. These as
sumptions help explain why a model can learn useful information even when only a
small portion of the dataset has labels. They are well-established in the literature on
semi-supervised learning [24, 26].
Smoothness assumption: This assumption states that if two data points are very
similar to each other (for example, two images of the same object taken from slightly
different angles), then their labels should also be similar. In other words, the model
should not drastically change its prediction when the input changes only slightly. Many
consistency-based SSL methods—such as Temporal Ensembling [27], Mean Teacher [3],
and UDA [6]—directly enforce this idea by checking whether the model gives consistent
predictions when the same image is modified through noise or augmentation.
Cluster assumption: This assumption suggests that real-world data tends to group
naturally into clusters. For example, images of cats cluster together because they share
similar visual patterns (fur textures, shapes, colours), while images of cars cluster in a
different region. SSL methods such as MixMatch [4], FixMatch [5], and FlexMatch [7]
make use of this structure by encouraging the model to assign the same label to points
in the same cluster. This means the model learns to place its decision between clusters,
not across them.
Low-density separation: This assumption builds further on the idea of clusters. It
states that the boundaries between different classes should lie in regions where there
are few data points. A simple way to imagine this is to picture two groups of points
on a graph: ideally, you want to draw a line between the groups where the space is
empty. Placing boundaries in high-density areas (full of points) risks separating points
that actually belong together. Methods such as Virtual Adversarial Training (VAT) [28]
and confidence-based pseudo-labelling [2, 8] follow this principle by avoiding uncertain
regions when learning from unlabelled data.
How Modern SSL Works
State-of-the-art SSL methods transform these assumptions into practical training strate
gies. Two of the most widely used strategies are consistency regularisation and pseudo
labelling.
Consistency regularisation: This technique is based on the smoothness assumption.
The idea is simple, when you slightly alter an image—by flipping it, adding noise, crop
ping it, or changing colours—the model should still recognise it as the same object.
Background
11
Consistency-based methods such as Temporal Ensembling [27], Mean Teacher [3], Fix
Match [5], and SimMatch [10] enforce this idea by training the model to produce similar
predictions across different augmented versions of the same image. This helps the model
become more stable and less sensitive to small changes.
Pseudo-labelling: In pseudo-labelling, the model predicts labels for unlabelled images
and then treats its most confident predictions as if they were true labels. This concept
was introduced in the Pseudo-Label method [2] and later became a foundation for ad
vanced SSL algorithms like MixMatch [4], FlexMatch [7], SoftMatch [9], and FreeMatch
[8]. However, pseudo-labelling can sometimes reinforce the model’s mistakes as if it
assigns an incorrect pseudo-label with high confidence and repeatedly trains on it, the
error can spread through the model. This issue is known as confirmation bias[25]. To
reduce this risk, modern methods use confidence thresholds and strong data augmenta
tion so that only reliable pseudo-labels are used. This process can be seen visualised in
2.4
Together, these techniques allow SSL models to learn from large amounts of unlabelled
data while avoiding common pitfalls such as unstable predictions or error amplification.
By leveraging the natural structure of real-world data, SSL can dramatically improve
performance in situations where labelled data is scarce, making it a powerful and prac
tical approach for modern machine-learning tasks.

4.4.3 Comparing Semi-Supervised Methods
The main goal of the evaluation is to compare the performance of several SSL algorithms
against a supervised baseline. The following comparison strategy will be used:
• Supervised Baseline: A standard supervised model trained only on the labelled
subset will serve as a baseline reference.
• SSL Algorithms: Algorithms such as Pseudo-Label [2], Temporal Ensembling
[27], Mean Teacher [3], MixMatch [4], FixMatch [5] and UDA [6] will be evaluated
under the same experimental conditions.
• Label Efficiency: For each method, accuracy will be measured at different label
fractions (e.g., 1%, 5%, 10% labelled data) to see how well each algorithm per
forms when labelled data is scarce, following recommendations from realistic SSL
evaluation work [25, 30].
The Evaluation Module will produce graphs that directly compare the performance of
different semi-supervised learning techniques using the metrics above. Examples include:
• Accuracy versus proportion of labelled data for all methods on a given dataset.
• Accuracy versus training, comparing convergence speed.
• Bar charts that summarise final test accuracy for each method and label budget.
These plots and tables will be complemented by qualitative analysis discussing why
certain methods perform better or worse, and how closely the results align with trends
reported in the literature.
4.4.4 Iterative Evaluation and Supervisor Feedback
For the development process, evaluation will be carried out iteratively. After each sprint.
i.e. big implementations (for example, after adding MixMatch or FixMatch), the current
results will be:
• Checked for correctness (e.g. verifying that accuracy and loss curves behave rea
sonably).
• Compared against the supervised baseline and, where possible, against published
ranges from existing SSL benchmarks [25, 30, 47].

Conclusions and Future Work
5.1 Discussion
This phase of the project involved developing a solid understanding of SSL, analysing
the literature, and designing an architecture capable of supporting multiple algorithms.
One of the most significant reflections from this stage is the complexity of modern
machine learning systems. Even before writing any code, a substantial amount of time
was required to understand the theoretical foundations, the logic behind different SSL
approaches, and the detailed mechanisms that make them work. Unlike traditional
software projects, machine learning work demands not only programming skills but also
mathematical reasoning and knowledge of data preprocessing techniques. Gaining this
understanding was essential before attempting any implementation.
Resource constraints also shaped the design choices. Many SSL methods assume access
to high-end GPUs and large batch sizes, which are not always available in a local devel
opment environment. This required planning for code that can be deployed on systems
with more resources.
Finally, defining a robust and meaningful evaluation strategy required a detailed analysis
of established SSL benchmarks. The literature consistently reports metrics such as top
1 accuracy, and test error across different labelled data budgets. Understanding not
just the definitions of these metrics, but also their purpose and how they reflect model
behaviour, was essential. Studying these evaluation practices provided clarity on how
to structure the benchmarking experiments later in the project and ensured that the
planned assessments align with the standards used in the SSL research community.
Overall, this phase underscored both the conceptual depth and practical complexity of
the machine learning domain.

5.2 Conclusion
From the work completed so far, several conclusions can be drawn:
• SSL is a well-motivated and active research area, with multiple state-of-the-art
methods showing substantial improvements over supervised learning when labelled
data is limited.
• Benchmark datasets such as CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, SVHN and STL-10 are appro
priate choices for this project, as they align with the datasets used by the original
SSL papers.
• Amodular architecture is essential for managing multiple algorithms and ensuring
reproducibility. The separation into data handling, model creation, training, and
evaluation facilitates experimentation and mitigates implementation risks.
• Awell-designed preprocessing and augmentation pipeline is critical for SSL success
and must be implemented carefully to match published approaches.
Overall, this phase has established the theoretical basis, requirements, and design direc
tion for the rest of the project.
5.3 Future Work
Several major areas of future work include:
• Exploring new variations of SSL techniques. Future research could involve
experimenting with small modifications to existing algorithms or testing alternative
training strategies to see whether performance can be improved under limited-label
conditions.
• Investigating deeper theoretical aspects of SSL. There is an opportunity
to study why certain SSL methods succeed or fail, how they behave on different
types of data, and what underlying assumptions most strongly influence their
performance.
• Evaluating SSL on larger or more diverse datasets. Extending experiments
beyond benchmark datasets to higher-resolution or domain-specific datasets—such
as medical, industrial, or real-world image collections—would help assess how well
SSL methods generalise.
